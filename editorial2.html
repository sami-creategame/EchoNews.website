Influencers Aren’t Replacing Journalism

Home Editorials
Editorial
Written by JABER Sami, On January 3, 2026

In the age of social media, TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube are no longer just entertainment platforms—they have become full-fledged information channels. Creators like Hugo Travers, founder of Hugo Décrypte, summarize daily news in short videos, reaching millions of young people who are often disconnected from traditional media.

According to the Pew Research Center, 53% of 18- to 30-year-olds get most of their news from social media, often from non-professional creators. As Lena Cruz, a professor of media ethics, points out, we tend to trust a familiar face more than a reliable source.

Does this mean influencers are replacing journalists? Not exactly. Journalism is built on fact-checking, cross-referencing sources, and providing context. Influencers, guided by the “attention economy,” prioritize speed and emotion—meaning mistakes can spread faster than corrections.

Journalism is not obsolete; it is evolving. Collaborations such as France Télévisions and Brut demonstrate that credibility and agility can coexist. Some universities even train creators in the fundamentals of fact-checking and journalistic ethics.

Influencers are replacing the method of delivery, but not the function. You can learn the basics of a subject on TikTok, but you cannot gain full expertise in fifteen seconds.

The challenge for us, as consumers, is clear: enjoy the speed of social media while always verifying information against reliable sources. Access to information is universal, but credibility still needs to be earned.
